When should I form an LLC?

Often later than people think — and sometimes never. Here's the honest trigger.

An LLC (limited liability company) is a legal structure that separates your business from you personally. It sounds essential the moment you earn a side dollar, but for many people it's premature. Here's when it actually earns its keep.

What an LLC does — and doesn't

  • Liability protection — its main benefit: it can shield your personal assets (home, savings) if the business is sued or owes debts.
  • It does not automatically save taxes. By default a single-member LLC is taxed just like a sole proprietor.

When it's usually worth it

  • You have real liability exposure — clients, contracts, employees, physical products, or property.
  • You have personal assets worth protecting.
  • Your business income is substantial and consistent.

When to wait

A tiny, occasional side hustle with little risk often doesn't need one yet — good insurance and clean records may be enough at first. You can form one when the business grows into it.

For families building or passing on wealth: Beyond liability, entity choice becomes a tax and planning lever as income rises — an S-corp election can reduce self-employment tax on higher profits, and holding companies or separate LLCs can segregate risk across ventures and real estate. This is squarely CPA-and-attorney territory; the savings and protection usually justify the advice well before they become obvious.

Bottom line: form an LLC when you have both real risk and real assets to protect — not simply because you made your first freelance dollar.
