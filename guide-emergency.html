How to build an emergency fund

The first thing to fund, because it's what keeps every other plan on track.

An emergency fund is cash set aside for the unexpected — a job loss, a medical bill, a car repair. It's the foundation of financial stability, because it's what stops a surprise from becoming a debt spiral.

How much

  • Start with a $1,000 starter fund — enough to handle most small emergencies without a credit card.
  • Build toward 3–6 months of essential expenses (needs, not your full budget).
  • Lean higher if your income is variable, you're self-employed, or you're the sole earner.

Size yours with the emergency-fund calculator.

Where to keep it

In a high-yield savings account — separate from checking so it isn't spent by accident, but accessible within a day or two. Not invested; this money's job is to be safe and available, not to grow.

How to build it

  • Automate a small transfer every payday — consistency beats size.
  • Route windfalls (tax refunds, bonuses) straight into it.
  • Treat it as a bill to yourself until it's funded, then shift that money to investing.
For families building or passing on wealth: Higher earners need a larger absolute cushion to cover their lifestyle, and often pair cash reserves with a line of credit for flexibility. The principle is unchanged: a liquid buffer prevents forced selling of investments at the worst possible moment — protecting the long-term plan from short-term shocks.
