Earning your first dollar

Money begins with contribution. Long before a child can budget or invest, they need to feel the first link in the chain: you do something useful, and you earn. Children who absorb that early tend to treat money as something they can create and control — not something that simply appears.

Set up the first earning

  • Choose one or two real jobs a child can genuinely own — feeding a pet, setting the table, sorting recycling.
  • Agree on a small, fixed amount, paid only when the work is really done.
  • Pay in cash they can hold and count. At this age, the physical act is the lesson.

It helps to keep allowance and earning a little separate. An allowance can teach managing money; earning teaches that money is produced by effort. Praise the effort and the follow-through, not the size of the pile.

For families building or passing on wealth: The earlier a child links money to contribution, the less it feels like something that just arrives. That one idea — wealth is earned and stewarded, not merely received — is the quiet foundation that helps a next generation handle whatever they inherit with confidence instead of entitlement.

Try this week: give your child one job that is truly theirs, and pay them for it by hand. Then ask what they would like to do with it — and resist steering the answer. The choice is where the learning lives.
