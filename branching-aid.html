Paying for college: aid, FAFSA, and loans

College is one of the first truly large financial decisions you'll make — and how you pay for it matters as much as where you go. The good news: there's a clear order to it, and a lot of money on the table if you ask.

Start with the FAFSA

The FAFSA (Free Application for Federal Student Aid) is the single most important form. It's free, and it unlocks grants, work-study, and federal loans — plus most state and school aid. File it as early as you can each year, even if you think you won't qualify; many families are surprised.

Know the order of "good" money

  • Grants and scholarships — free money you never repay. Chase these first, relentlessly.
  • Work-study — a part-time job built into your aid.
  • Federal student loans — if you must borrow, start here. Subsidized loans don't charge interest while you're in school; unsubsidized ones do.
  • Private loans — last resort. Fewer protections and no income-driven repayment.

The one rule for borrowing

A simple guideline keeps loans from following you for decades: don't borrow more in total than you expect to earn in your first year after graduating. Stay at or below that, and repayment usually stays manageable.

For families building or passing on wealth: Even when a family can pay outright, filing the FAFSA and comparing net cost is worth it \u2014 merit aid isnt always need-based. A 529 plan grows tax-free for education, and paying tuition directly to the school is exempt from gift tax, making it a tax-efficient way for parents or grandparents to help fund it.
