Lease or buy a car?

Leasing looks cheaper monthly and usually costs more over time. Here's the trade.

Leasing and buying answer different questions. Leasing minimizes the monthly payment and keeps you in a newer car; buying costs more up front but builds toward owning something outright.

The core trade-off

  • Leasing — lower monthly payments, but you never own the car and you're effectively renting it forever, with mileage limits and wear charges. Payments never end if you keep leasing.
  • Buying — higher payments (or cash), but once the loan is paid off you drive payment-free for years, and the car is an asset you can sell.

Which fits you

  • Lease if you want a new car every few years and don't drive many miles — or if you can write it off as a legitimate business expense.
  • Buy (and keep it) for the lowest long-run cost — the paid-off years are where the savings live.

For most people optimizing for cost, buying a reliable car and keeping it well past the loan wins. Leasing wins on convenience and cash flow, not on total cost.

For families building or passing on wealth: For business owners, a lease or the business purchase of a vehicle can carry tax advantages worth modeling with a CPA. As a personal-finance decision, though, the long-run math still favors buying and holding — leasings appeal is lifestyle and cash flow, not wealth-building.
