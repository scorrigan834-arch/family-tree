Social Security basics

A foundation, not a full plan — and when you claim changes the check for life.

Social Security pays you a monthly benefit in retirement, based on your lifetime earnings. It's a valuable foundation — but it's designed to replace only part of your income, so it works best as one leg of a plan, not the whole thing.

How it works

  • You earn "credits" by working and paying FICA taxes; about 10 years of work qualifies you.
  • Your benefit is based on your highest 35 years of earnings.
  • You can claim as early as 62, at full retirement age (66–67 for most), or as late as 70.

The claiming decision

This is the big one. Claiming early permanently reduces your monthly check; delaying past full retirement age raises it by roughly 8% per year until 70. If you expect a long life and can afford to wait, delaying often pays off substantially. Married couples have added strategies around spousal and survivor benefits worth coordinating.

For families building or passing on wealth: For higher earners, Social Security replaces a smaller share of pre-retirement income, so it matters less to the monthly budget — but the claiming decision still involves real money and tax interactions. Delaying can act like a low-cost, inflation-adjusted annuity that protects a surviving spouse; its worth modeling with an advisor alongside your other income sources.

Bottom line: treat Social Security as the stable base of retirement income and build your savings on top of it. Check your estimated benefit anytime at ssa.gov.
