How banks and interest work

A bank is a safe place to keep money — far safer than a drawer — and, kept in the right account, it can even grow a little while it simply sits there.

What "interest" really means

When you keep money in a savings account, the bank pays you a small amount for the privilege of holding it. That payment is called interest. It looks tiny at first — a few cents — but here's the powerful part: next time, you earn interest on your original money and on the interest you already earned. Money begins making money. Over years and decades, that snowball becomes one of the strongest forces in personal finance.

A good first step

Ask a parent about opening a youth or teen savings account, ideally one with no monthly fee. Watching real interest land — even a few cents a month — turns an abstract idea into something you've actually seen happen.
