Investing for the first time

The biggest advantage you have in your twenties isn't money — it's time. Money invested early has decades to compound, and compounding does the heavy lifting: a modest amount invested now can outgrow a much larger amount invested later.

A sensible order to start

  • If your employer offers a 401(k) match, contribute at least enough to get the full match — it's an immediate, guaranteed return you shouldn't leave behind.
  • Open a Roth IRA — a straightforward retirement account whose growth can be withdrawn tax-free in retirement.
  • Favor low-cost index funds, which spread your money across the whole market instead of betting on single companies.

You don't need to predict the market or pick winners. Steady, automatic contributions left alone for years are what work. Try the retirement projection to see how much of your future balance is contributions versus growth — the answer surprises most people.

For families building or passing on wealth: If youll one day help steward family assets, the fastest way to prepare is to invest your own money first. Understanding risk, time horizon, fees, and market ups-and-downs from experience — even with small amounts — is what lets you engage as a partner with a family advisor rather than a bystander.
