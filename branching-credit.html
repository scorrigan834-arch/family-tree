Student loans and credit

Borrowing money means paying to use someone else's money for a while. The price is interest, shown as an APR (annual percentage rate). The higher the APR, the more that borrowing costs you — which is why the same purchase can quietly cost far more on a credit card than in cash.

Two rules that protect you

  • Borrow only what you truly need — especially with student loans. Every dollar borrowed is more than a dollar to repay, so treat the loan amount as a real decision, not a default.
  • On your first credit card, pay the full balance every month. Do that and you build a strong credit history essentially for free. Carry a balance and high interest starts working against you fast.

Why credit matters later

Your credit score is a track record of how reliably you repay. Good habits now make renting an apartment, getting a loan at a fair rate, and sometimes even landing a job noticeably easier. It's one of the few things where small, boring consistency pays off for decades.
