New vs used car

A car is a depreciating asset. The buying decision is mostly about who absorbs that drop.

A car loses value the moment you drive it off the lot, and keeps losing it for years. That single fact — depreciation — drives most of the new-vs-used math.

The depreciation reality

A new car can lose roughly 20% of its value in the first year and around half within five years. Buying a 2–4 year-old used car lets someone else absorb that steepest drop while you still get a modern, reliable vehicle — often the sweet spot for value.

When new can make sense

  • You'll keep the car a very long time (a decade-plus), spreading the depreciation thin.
  • Low-rate financing or warranties meaningfully change the math.
  • You value the latest safety technology and want it now.

Either way, buy sensibly

  • A common guideline: keep total car costs (payment, insurance, fuel, upkeep) under ~15–20% of take-home pay.
  • Reliability and total cost of ownership matter more than the badge.
For families building or passing on wealth: Cars are consumption, not investment, at every level of wealth — which is why many wealthy, financially disciplined people quietly drive reliable vehicles for years. Spending restraint on depreciating assets is one of the clearest habits that separates lasting wealth from high income.
