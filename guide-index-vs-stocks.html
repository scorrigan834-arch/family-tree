Index funds vs individual stocks: what the data says

Picking winners is thrilling and, for most people, a losing game. Here's why.

It's tempting to build wealth by picking great companies. The trouble is the evidence: over long periods, the large majority of professional fund managers — full-time experts with vast resources — fail to beat a simple, low-cost index fund after fees. If the pros mostly can't, part-time individual pickers face steep odds.

Why index funds win for most people

  • Instant diversification — one fund can hold hundreds or thousands of companies, so no single failure sinks you.
  • Low fees — a fraction of active-fund costs, and fees compound against you over decades.
  • No guesswork — you own the whole market's growth instead of betting on which slice wins.
  • Behavior-friendly — less temptation to tinker means fewer costly mistakes.

Is there a place for individual stocks?

Yes — a small one. Many sensible investors keep a low-cost index core as the foundation and, if they enjoy it, put a modest amount (often 5–10%) into researched individual stocks. The key is that this is the satellite, not the core, and it's money you can afford to be wrong about.

For families building or passing on wealth: Large portfolios dont require exotic complexity to do well — a diversified, low-cost core plus disciplined rebalancing is a foundation that has served serious investors for decades. Complexity and high fees often subtract value rather than add it; simplicity, consistency, and tax-awareness tend to win.

Bottom line: for the money that matters, boring and diversified beats clever and concentrated. Try the compound-interest calculator to see how low fees and steady growth compound.
