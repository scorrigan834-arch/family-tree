Capital gains taxes

What you owe when investments grow — and the timing that cuts the bill.

When you sell an investment for more than you paid, the profit is a capital gain — and how it's taxed depends heavily on how long you held it.

Short-term vs long-term

  • Short-term (held one year or less) is taxed as ordinary income — the same high rates as your paycheck.
  • Long-term (held more than a year) is taxed at lower rates — 0%, 15%, or 20% for most people. Simply crossing the one-year mark can dramatically cut the tax.

Gains are taxed only when you sell (realize) them. Investments you keep holding grow untaxed — one reason long-term, low-turnover investing is so tax-efficient.

Two useful moves

  • Hold for the long term when you can, for the lower rate.
  • Tax-loss harvesting — selling a loser to offset gains — can reduce what you owe.
  • Gains inside a Roth or 401(k) aren't taxed the same way, which is why tax-advantaged accounts are so powerful.
For families building or passing on wealth: This is where planning gets valuable. A "step-up in basis" resets an assets cost basis at death, so heirs may owe little or no capital-gains tax on a lifetime of growth. Gifting appreciated stock to charity or a donor-advised fund avoids the gain entirely while providing a deduction. Coordinated with a CPA, these strategies can save families significant sums.
