The gap

The most expensive gap in wealth planning.

We are living through the largest transfer of wealth in history. An estimated $124 trillion will change hands through 2048, with more than $100 trillion flowing to heirs. Yet the financial industry has spent decades perfecting how to prepare estates — and almost no time preparing the people who inherit them.

The results are stark. Most heirs leave their family’s advisor after inheriting: research finds only about one in four plan to keep that advisor, and just one in five among those who already have. And the wealth itself often doesn’t survive — commonly cited estimates put the loss at roughly 70% by the second generation and 90% by the third.

The cause usually isn’t bad investing. It’s that heirs were never prepared — never brought into the conversation, never taught to steward what they would receive, and often without any relationship to the advisor at all.

The industry learned to prepare assets for heirs. It never learned to prepare heirs for assets.

That is the gap Family Tree is built to close: the education layer that prepares people, not just portfolios — decades before the transfer, not after.

Figures: Cerulli Associates (2024–2025) and BlackRock advisor insights. Wealth-erosion estimates reflect widely cited multigenerational studies. Sources: BlackRock, CNBC / Cerulli.

For advisors & wealth-management firms

Engage the next generation before you inherit the problem.

The strongest predictor of keeping assets through a wealth transfer is a genuine relationship with the whole family, built early. Family Tree gives you a credible, white-glove way to build that relationship — and to be seen doing the thing most firms only talk about: preparing the heirs.

What it does for your practice

  • Next-generation retention. Give client families a resource their children actually use, so your relationship with the heirs begins years before the transfer — not in the estate-settlement meeting.
  • A differentiator beyond performance. Financial education for a client’s children is a tangible, memorable demonstration that you care for the whole family, not just the portfolio.
  • An on-ramp to the hard conversations. Legacy and values discussions are emotional and tough to start. A shared, neutral resource gives families a natural way in.
  • The complement to your estate work. You prepare the estate and the plan; Family Tree helps prepare the people who will live with it — the missing half of a complete plan.

What it does for their heirs

  • They inherit judgment, not just assets. Age-appropriate financial capability so the next generation can hold what it receives.
  • They arrive fluent. Heirs who already understand accounts, investing, and estate basics come to the table as partners in the relationship, not strangers to it.
  • They learn the “why.” The values and purpose behind the wealth — which research shows are what actually preserve it across generations.
  • You become the family’s advisor. Across generations, not just the current client’s lifetime.

For your clients — families building or passing on wealth

Raise a capable steward, not an entitled inheritor.

The old proverb — shirtsleeves to shirtsleeves in three generations — isn’t about bad luck. It’s about heirs handed wealth without the preparation to hold it. Family Tree is designed to close that gap inside your own family, one age-appropriate step at a time.

What’s in it for you

  • A path from age 5 to 50. The money conversation becomes a decade-by-decade upbringing — not one overwhelming talk at twenty-five.
  • The wealth-specific threads, woven in. Stewardship over entitlement, generosity and philanthropy, the basics of trusts and estates, and learning to work with advisors as a partner.
  • A neutral place for hard conversations. A trusted, shared starting point for talking about money, values, and what you hope to pass on — beyond the balance sheet.
  • A complement to your advisor and attorney. The education layer beneath the plan they build — never a replacement for it.

What’s in it for your children

  • They avoid the entitlement trap — because they learned early that money is earned, stewarded, and used with intention.
