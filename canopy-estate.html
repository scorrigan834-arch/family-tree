Estate planning and what to pass on

Estate planning isn't only for the wealthy, and it isn't morbid — it's simply how you make sure what you've built goes where you intend, with as little stress and cost as possible for the people you love.

The basics to have in place

  • A will that states who receives what, and who would care for any minor children.
  • Up-to-date beneficiaries on retirement accounts and insurance — these override your will, so they matter enormously.
  • A power of attorney and health directive naming who can act for you if you can't.
  • For larger or more complex estates, trusts can control how and when assets pass on, reduce taxes, and protect privacy. A qualified estate attorney is worth it here.

The part most families miss

Studies of family wealth repeatedly find the same thing: money is far more often lost across generations because heirs weren't prepared than because the planning was wrong. The documents matter — but preparing the people matters more.

For families building or passing on wealth: The most valuable inheritance isnt the balance sheet — its the judgment to handle it. Talk with your heirs early and openly about values, purpose, and how money is meant to be used. Bring them into the conversation with your advisors before they need to be. Heirs who understand the "why" behind the wealth are dramatically more likely to preserve it — and that preparation is the entire reason Family Tree exists.
