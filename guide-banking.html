Banking basics: accounts that work for you

The right two accounts, no fees, and a savings account that actually pays.

Banking is the plumbing of your financial life, and getting it right is mostly about avoiding needless fees and using an account that actually pays you. Two accounts cover most needs.

Checking and savings

  • Checking — everyday money in and out.
  • Savings — money you're setting aside. Use a high-yield savings account (HYSA), which pays meaningfully more interest than a standard savings account for the same safety and access.

Avoid the fees that quietly add up

  • Choose accounts with no monthly fee and no minimum balance.
  • Turn off overdraft "coverage" or link a savings buffer to dodge overdraft fees.
  • Use in-network ATMs, or a bank that reimburses fees.

Banks vs credit unions

Banks are for-profit; credit unions are member-owned nonprofits that often offer lower fees and better rates, with fewer branches. Online banks frequently pay the highest savings rates thanks to lower overhead. All are safe if deposits are FDIC-insured (banks) or NCUA-insured (credit unions) — up to $250,000 per depositor, per institution.

For families building or passing on wealth: As cash balances grow, that $250,000 insurance limit starts to matter. Families holding significant cash spread it across institutions, use banks with sweep programs that extend coverage, or move excess into short-term Treasuries and money-market funds — keeping cash both safe and productive rather than idle.

Do this: if your savings sits in a big-bank account paying near 0%, moving it to a reputable HYSA is one of the easiest upgrades in personal finance.
