Money and mental health

Money stress is real, common, and treatable. A healthy relationship with money is part of the plan.

Money and mental health run in both directions: financial stress harms wellbeing, and anxiety or depression can make managing money harder. Naming that link — and treating it kindly — is part of a healthy financial life, not separate from it.

Common patterns

  • Avoidance — not opening bills or checking accounts because it's stressful, which quietly makes things worse.
  • Anxiety — constant worry about money even when the numbers are okay.
  • Emotional spending — shopping to soothe difficult feelings.

Gentle, practical steps

  • Automate the essentials so staying on track doesn't depend on willpower on a hard day.
  • Look, briefly and regularly — a short weekly check-in reduces the dread of not knowing.
  • Focus on the next small action, not the whole mountain.
  • Talk to someone — a trusted person, a nonprofit credit counselor, or a therapist.
For families building or passing on wealth: Money anxiety isnt only a problem of scarcity — significant wealth can bring its own stress: fear of losing it, guilt, pressure, or a sense of not having earned it (common among heirs). A healthy relationship with money — clarity, purpose, and a plan — matters at every level, and is a large part of what preparing the next generation really means.

If you're struggling, that's worth taking seriously. Support — from a trusted person, a licensed professional, or a nonprofit financial counselor — can make a real difference.
