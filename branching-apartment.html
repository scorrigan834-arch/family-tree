Your first apartment

Your first place of your own is exciting — and it's also your first real lesson in the true cost of housing. The rent is only the headline number.

What you can actually afford

A common guideline is to keep rent at or below about 30% of your take-home pay. Go much higher and everything else — food, transport, saving — gets squeezed. Use the take-home-pay calculator to start from your real number, not your salary.

The upfront costs nobody warns you about

  • Security deposit — often one month's rent, sometimes more.
  • First (and sometimes last) month due at signing.
  • Application and setup fees, plus utilities that may need deposits.

Budget for moving in to cost two to three times the monthly rent all at once.

Protect yourself

  • Read the lease before signing — term length, what breaking it costs, who pays which utilities, pet and guest rules.
  • Get renters insurance. It's usually cheap and covers your belongings and some liability.
  • With roommates, put who-pays-what in writing. Money is the top thing that ends good roommate situations.
