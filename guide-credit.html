How credit scores work

One number opens or closes doors for decades. Here's what moves it.

Your credit score is a snapshot of how reliably you repay borrowed money. It quietly shapes whether you're approved for a loan, the rate you pay, and sometimes whether you can rent an apartment — so understanding it pays off for years.

What actually makes up the score

  • Payment history (~35%) — paying on time is the single biggest factor; one missed payment can sting for years.
  • Utilization (~30%) — how much of your available credit you're using. Keeping balances well below 30% (lower is better) helps.
  • Length of history (~15%) — older accounts help, so don't close your oldest card casually.
  • Credit mix (~10%) and new inquiries (~10%) — variety of accounts, and not opening several at once.

How to build it

  • Pay every bill on time, automatically.
  • Keep utilization low; request limit increases you don't then use.
  • Keep old accounts open.
  • Check reports free at annualcreditreport.com and dispute errors.

Two myths to drop: checking your own score doesn't hurt it, and carrying a balance does not help your score — it just costs interest.

For families building or passing on wealth: Good credit matters at every level of wealth — it shapes the terms on mortgages, investment-property loans, and business financing, where a fraction of a percent compounds into large sums. Its one of the few valuable assets built purely through consistent habits.
