Should I start investing now?

Almost always yes — but a couple of things come first.

The best time to start investing is usually now, because time is the ingredient compounding needs most. But two things generally come before you put money in the market.

Two things first

  • Pay off high-interest debt. Clearing a 20% credit card is a guaranteed 20% return — better than you can reliably earn investing.
  • Build a starter emergency fund. A few months of expenses in cash means a surprise won't force you to sell investments at a bad time. Size it with the emergency-fund calculator.

Then start — simply

  • Capture any 401(k) employer match first; it's free money.
  • Open a Roth IRA and buy a low-cost, broad index fund.
  • Automate a regular contribution — consistency beats timing.

You don't need a lot to begin; you need to begin. Small amounts invested early, left alone, outgrow larger amounts invested later — see the compound-interest calculator.

Don't wait for the "right" moment

Timing the market is a loser's game even for professionals. Time in the market matters far more than timing it; the cost of waiting for certainty usually beats the cost of a bumpy start.

For families building or passing on wealth: If you already have assets, the question shifts from whether to invest to how — asset allocation, tax-location across account types, and diversification. But the foundations dont change: control debt, hold a cash reserve, keep costs low, and let compounding work. Complexity should serve the plan, not replace the basics.
